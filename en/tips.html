Cost structure

You have more costs than you think

Our starting point: you always have more business costs than you think. Walk through your day mentally:

  • You get up and check your calendar on your phone. Costs: phone (investment) + subscription.
  • Over breakfast you work through some e-mails. Costs: laptop (investment) + your Microsoft Office subscription. Own domain? Then the hosting costs of your e-mail (and website) are business-deductible too.
  • You get on your bike, scooter or motorcycle, or into your car or the train. All business costs, and often investments. Bought your transport privately? Then you may apply the kilometre allowance. Use our Excel model for tracking your kilometres — just ask us for it.
  • You have a lunch meeting with a (potential) client. Costs: the lunch. Entertainment expenses if you sit down, canteen costs if you grab a sandwich to go.
  • After lunch you buy a nice bottle of wine for a client who just took a big step. Costs: business gift.

That's how you walk through your day, and every time you spend on business: pay from your business account and upload the receipt or invoice PDF straight into the SnelStart Purchases portal.

Investments

December or January?

The word "investment" came up several times in the cost structure tip, and not without reason: if you invest more than a certain amount in a fiscal year, we can apply extra deductions for you.

Practical tip: want to buy something in December, but you won't reach the threshold this year? Then wait until January. You'll have a whole year left to possibly make another investment and reach the threshold after all.

You'll find the current threshold at the Dutch tax authorities' page on the small-scale investment deduction (KIA) — click through to the relevant year there (page in Dutch).

Pay on time

Instant isn't always instant

Always pay your business taxes well in time. Within the Netherlands, money is in principle debited and credited instantly, but administratively that's not always the case.

You've probably seen it: you pay by card at the Albert Heijn and later the amount shows up in your bank statement dated the next day. It works the same way at the Dutch tax authorities. Transfer the money on the very last day, and administratively that can trigger a fine.

So pay on time, or use the iDEAL/Wero payment feature in SnelStart.

Payment dates

Put them in your calendar now

At the start of the year, put all payment dates in your calendar:

  • VAT returns: pay before the end of the month following the quarter. For Q1, that means end of April.
  • Payroll taxes: pay before the end of the following month. Payroll taxes for January are paid before the end of February.
  • VAT correction return (the year-end suppletie): pay when invited by the tax authorities. That invitation usually arrives about a month after we've filed your return. But beware: this is entirely up to the tax authorities. In theory the payment request can arrive the very next day. Factor this into your liquidity position.
  • Corporate and personal income tax: pay when invited by the tax authorities, usually a few months after filing. Here too: in theory the request can arrive the next day. Keep it in mind for your liquidity.

Provisional assessment

An advance on the bill

The provisional assessment is an advance on the bill, so to speak, and only applies to income tax and corporate income tax. The tax authorities often send one on their own initiative, but base it on your latest filed return. So your advance may well be based on figures from one and a half to two years ago. And a lot can have changed in the meantime.

You can therefore always submit a request to change your provisional assessment. In many cases the tax authorities agree, but it's not guaranteed.

Does your situation change significantly during the year? Let us know and we'll request the adjustment for you.

Tax interest

Avoid the unpleasant surprise

On tax that is paid later than the official filing date, the Dutch tax authorities charge tax interest — for example when an extension has been requested for you. The exception: the amount you've already paid during the year through your provisional assessment.

The percentage used to be low, because interest rates across Europe were low for a long time. Now that rates are rising, tax interest rises with them. If you don't want to lose a lot of money to interest, ask us to request a provisional assessment for you.

And note: if your situation changes significantly during the year, tell us and ask us to adjust the provisional assessment. That way you won't face an unpleasant surprise when we discuss your return with you.

Filing extension

An extra year of time

The regular deadlines are 1 May (income tax) and 1 June (corporate income tax). Because we have to spread our work across the whole year, the Dutch tax authorities have given us permission to apply for an extension: an extra year to file our clients' returns. We make use of that.

Some people want their return done as soon as possible, others prefer to wait as long as they can. Let us know your preference and we'll take it into account in our planning.

Income tax checklist

What do we need from you?

The same question every year, so here it is in black and white: this is what we need from you for a standard income tax return. Not everything applies to you; just send in what does.

  • Annual income statements (jaaropgaven) for you and your tax partner (if employed alongside your business)
  • Provisional assessments or refunds received during the year
  • Your own home: the WOZ decision and the annual mortgage statement
  • Balances of bank and savings accounts, investments and crypto as of 1 January — including those of your children under 18
  • Annuity premiums paid and premiums for your occupational disability insurance
  • Healthcare costs that weren't reimbursed
  • Donations to charities
  • Partner alimony received or paid
  • Details of your tax partner and children (names and dates of birth), if we don't have them yet
  • (BV) Dividend payments and loans from your own BV

Not sure whether something is relevant? Just send it along or ask. Better one document too many than one too few.

Handy tip: grab last year's return and, for everything that was declared then, look up this year's documents. In many cases that covers almost the entire return.
